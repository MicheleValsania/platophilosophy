The Soul

Tripartition, immortality, and the chariot allegory · Reading time: 4-5 minutes

What is the soul (psyche)? For Plato, it is not merely a life-force or consciousness. The soul is the essence of a person—immortal, rational, and the seat of virtue. Understanding the soul is central to understanding Plato's ethics, psychology, and metaphysics.

The Tripartite Soul

In the Republic, Plato argues the soul has three parts, each with its own function and desire:

1. Reason (logistikon)

The rational part. Seeks truth and wisdom. Should rule the soul. Corresponds to the philosopher class in the ideal state. Located in the head.

2. Spirit (thumoeid es)

The spirited part. Loves honor, victory, and recognition. Ally of reason when properly trained. Corresponds to the guardian class. Located in the chest.

3. Appetite (epithumetikon)

The desiring part. Seeks bodily pleasures—food, drink, sex, wealth. Must be controlled by reason. Corresponds to the producer class. Located in the belly.

A just soul is one where reason rules, spirit supports reason, and appetite obeys. An unjust soul is disordered—appetite or spirit dominating reason.

The Chariot Allegory

In the Phaedrus, Plato offers a vivid image: the soul is like a charioteer (reason) driving two horses. One horse (spirit) is noble, obedient, and loves honor. The other (appetite) is wild, unruly, and pulls toward base pleasures.

The charioteer must control both horses, guiding them toward the vision of the Forms. The difficulty of this task explains moral struggle—our souls are constantly torn between higher and lower impulses.

"The soul is like a chariot with a charioteer and two horses, one noble and good, the other quite the opposite. So of necessity, the driving is difficult and troublesome."
—Plato, Phaedrus 246a-b

Immortality of the Soul

Plato offers multiple arguments that the soul is immortal:

  • Cyclical Argument (Phaedo): Everything comes from its opposite. Life from death, death from life. Therefore souls exist before and after bodily life.
  • Recollection Argument: We remember Forms from before birth. This presupposes the soul's pre-existence.
  • Affinity Argument: The soul is like the Forms—invisible, unchanging. The body is like physical things—visible, changing. What is unchanging cannot cease to exist.
  • Self-Motion Argument (Phaedrus): The soul is self-moving. What moves itself is the source of motion and cannot cease to move (to be).

Transmigration and Reincarnation

Plato believed in reincarnation. After death, the soul is judged. Virtuous souls may join the gods; vicious souls are punished. Eventually, souls return to new bodies. The life you live determines your next incarnation—philosophers may become gods, tyrants may become animals.

The Myth of Er (Republic X) describes this process: souls choosing their next lives based on wisdom or ignorance gained in previous incarnations.

Care of the Soul

If the soul is immortal and what truly matters, we should prioritize its health over the body's pleasures. Philosophy is the practice of dying—separating the soul from bodily concerns, preparing it for pure existence among the Forms.

"The one aim of those who practice philosophy in the proper manner is to practice for dying and death."
—Plato, Phaedo 64a
