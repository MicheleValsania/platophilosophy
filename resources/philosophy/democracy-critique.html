Democracy Critique

Why Plato distrusted democracy and majority rule · Reading time: 4-5 minutes

Plato witnessed democracy's darkest hour: Athens, the world's first democracy, executed his beloved teacher Socrates. This trauma shaped Plato's political philosophy. In the Republic, he offers a devastating critique of democracy, arguing it inevitably degenerates into tyranny.

Why Plato Hated Democracy

For Plato, democracy commits a fundamental error: it assumes all opinions are equally valid. It treats governing as if anyone can do it, like any other skill can be practiced by amateurs. But ruling requires expertise—knowledge of the Good.

Would you let just anyone navigate a ship? Would you elect your surgeon by popular vote? No—you'd trust the expert. Why, then, trust the ignorant masses to govern?

The Ship of State

Plato's famous metaphor: imagine a ship where the crew fights over who should navigate, though none have studied navigation. They think sailing is just opinion—anyone's view is as good as another's. The true navigator, who has knowledge of stars and currents, is mocked as impractical.

This is democracy. We let everyone vote on complex issues—justice, economics, war—when most have only opinions, not knowledge.

"Until philosophers rule, or those now called kings genuinely philosophize, cities will have no rest from evils."
—Plato, Republic V, 473c-d

Democracy as Soul-Disorder

Just as the tripartite soul can be disordered (appetite or spirit overthrowing reason), so can cities. Democracy is the city where appetite rules—the desires of the masses dictate policy.

In democracy:

  • All desires are treated as equally valid
  • "Freedom" means pursuing any appetite without restraint
  • No hierarchy of better and worse ways of life
  • Everyone is "equal," regardless of wisdom or virtue
  • The wise are mocked for being "elitist"

This seems pleasant at first—everyone does what they want. But it's chaos. The city lacks direction, principle, coherence.

From Democracy to Tyranny

Plato argues democracy inevitably degenerates into tyranny. Here's how:

  1. Excessive freedom: Everyone pursues desires without limit. No discipline, no respect for authority.
  2. Faction: Demagogues arise, promising to satisfy the masses' appetites. They attack the wealthy as enemies of the people.
  3. Conflict: The wealthy resist redistribution. Class warfare intensifies.
  4. The "champion of the people": One demagogue defeats the others, becomes the people's champion.
  5. Consolidation of power: The champion demands bodyguards for "protection." He eliminates rivals.
  6. Tyranny: The champion becomes tyrant—unlimited power, no law, ruling by fear. The people, in their desire for freedom, enslaved themselves.

Plato saw this pattern in Greek history. Many democracies had become tyrannies. He predicted it would happen again.

The Democratic Man

Plato describes the "democratic soul"—the person raised in democracy:

  • Treats all desires as equal—studying philosophy, drinking wine, making money, exercising—all equally "valid"
  • No stable values or commitments
  • Drifts from pleasure to pleasure
  • "Diversity" of desires, but no hierarchy
  • Believes he's free, but is enslaved by whims

This person thinks freedom means doing whatever you want. But true freedom, for Plato, means reason ruling the soul—liberation from tyranny of appetite.

Socrates' Execution

Democracy killed Socrates. Why? Because Socrates questioned, challenged, examined. He made people uncomfortable. The democratic mob, in their freedom, voted to silence him.

This is democracy's danger: the majority can be wrong. Popular opinion is not truth. The masses can be swayed by rhetoric, passion, self-interest.

Modern Democracy vs. Plato's Critique

Does Plato's critique apply to modern democracies?

Yes:

  • Voters are often ignorant of policy details
  • Demagogues manipulate emotions, win elections
  • Short-term desires override long-term wisdom
  • Expertise is distrusted ("elite" is an insult)
  • Some democracies have become tyrannies (Weimar → Nazi Germany)

No:

  • Modern democracies have checks and balances Plato's Athens lacked
  • Constitutional protections prevent majority tyranny
  • Representative democracy (not direct) mitigates mob rule
  • Education and free press allow informed citizenry
  • Democracy, despite flaws, has proven stable and prosperous

Plato's Alternative

Plato doesn't oppose all rule by the people—he opposes rule by the ignorant people. If everyone were philosopher-kings, democracy would be ideal. But they're not.

His solution: rule by the wise. Epistocracy (rule by knowers) rather than democracy (rule by the people).

The Enduring Question

Should we trust experts or the people? Wisdom or freedom? Plato says wisdom. Democrats say freedom. Perhaps the truth lies in balancing both—constitutional democracy with educated citizens and expert advisors.

But Plato's challenge remains: if you wouldn't trust the mob to pilot a ship, why trust them to pilot a state?
