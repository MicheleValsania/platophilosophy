The Ideal State

Plato's Republic—justice, classes, and the kallipolis · Reading time: 4-5 minutes

In the Republic, Plato constructs an ideal city—kallipolis (beautiful city)—to examine justice "writ large." By studying justice in a city, he hopes to understand justice in the soul. The result is one of philosophy's most influential (and controversial) political visions.

The Three Classes

Plato's ideal state has three classes, corresponding to the three parts of the soul:

1. Rulers (Philosopher-Kings)

Function: Govern with wisdom
Virtue: Wisdom
Soul part: Reason
Education: 50 years of training, culminating in vision of the Good
Property: None (communism)
Family: None (communism of spouses and children)

2. Guardians (Auxiliaries)

Function: Defend the city
Virtue: Courage
Soul part: Spirit
Education: Music and gymnastics, military training
Property: None (communism)
Family: None (communism)

3. Producers (Farmers, Craftsmen, Merchants)

Function: Provide economic goods
Virtue: Temperance (accepting their role)
Soul part: Appetite
Education: Training in crafts
Property: Yes (can own, accumulate)
Family: Yes (traditional families)

Justice in the City

Justice is each class performing its proper function without interfering with the others. The city is just when:

  • Philosopher-kings rule (using wisdom)
  • Guardians enforce their decisions (using courage)
  • Producers obey and produce (exercising temperance)
  • No class usurps another's role

Injustice occurs when classes overstep—merchants trying to rule, soldiers disobeying, etc. This mirrors injustice in the soul (appetite or spirit overthrowing reason).

The Noble Lie (Myth of the Metals)

To maintain harmony, Plato proposes a "noble lie": citizens will be told that the gods mixed gold into philosopher-kings, silver into guardians, and bronze/iron into producers. This myth justifies the class structure as natural and divinely ordained.

However, class is determined by nature and education, not birth. Children are tested; a bronze-souled child of gold parents becomes a producer, and vice versa. So there's social mobility based on merit.

Communism of Property and Family

The most shocking features of Plato's city:

  • Guardians and rulers own nothing: No private property, no money, no possessions beyond necessities. This prevents corruption—they can't rule for personal gain.
  • No traditional families for guardians: Marriages are temporary, arranged by rulers for eugenic purposes (breeding the best guardians). Children are raised communally, don't know their biological parents.
  • Women guardians: Radical for ancient Greece! Women can be guardians and philosopher-kings if they have the right nature. Plato argues ability, not sex, determines class.

These measures ensure guardians focus solely on the city's good, not personal interests.

"Until philosophers rule as kings, or those now called kings genuinely philosophize, and political power and philosophy coincide—there will be no rest from evils for cities, nor I think for the human race."
—Plato, Republic V, 473c-d

Education in the Ideal City

Education is crucial for producing just citizens:

  • Early childhood (0-18): Music (poetry, arts, stories) and gymnastics. Strict censorship—no Homer's lies about gods. Goal: Form character.
  • Ages 18-20: Military training. Test for courage. Many stop here, become auxiliaries.
  • Ages 20-30: Mathematics (arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, harmonics). Abstract thinking. Preparation for philosophy.
  • Ages 30-35: Dialectic (pure philosophy). Dangerous if done too young—becomes sophistry. Study of Forms.
  • Ages 35-50: Return to practical life. Hold offices, gain experience. Test virtue in real world.
  • Age 50+: Philosopher-kings. Alternate between contemplating Forms and ruling.

Is This City Possible?

Plato himself questions whether the ideal city can exist. At best, it's a model in heaven, a standard to judge actual cities. It may not be realizable in practice—but it shows the principle: cities should be ruled by wisdom, organized for justice, not self-interest.

Modern Criticisms

  • Totalitarian?: Karl Popper argued Plato's city is totalitarian—no freedom, no privacy, eugenic breeding, censorship.
  • Impractical: Abolishing family? Philosopher-kings ruling reluctantly? Guards with no property? Never been done.
  • Who guards the guardians?: What prevents philosopher-kings from corruption? Why trust them?
  • Elitism: Most people (producers) excluded from political power. Is this just?
  • Gender equality?: Plato's view is advanced for his time, but still treats women as weaker versions of men.

Defense of Plato

Perhaps Plato's point is not a literal blueprint but a thought experiment: if we want a perfectly just city, then this is what it requires. And maybe that's impossible—which shows how difficult justice is.

Or perhaps he's critiquing democracy by showing the gap between how cities are ruled (by popular opinion, self-interest) and how they should be ruled (by wisdom, for the common good).
