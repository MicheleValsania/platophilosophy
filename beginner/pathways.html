Choose Your Path

You have completed the beginner's path. You now understand why philosophy exists as a discipline, what makes Plato distinctive, how the dialogue form works, and why reading primary texts matters.

Where you go next depends on how you prefer to learn. Here are three pathways:

PATH 1

The Historical Path

If you want to understand the context before the text. Who was Plato? Why did he write? How did his time shape his thought?

Start: Athens in 428 BC → View Timeline

12 pages · ~40 minutes total reading

PATH 2

The Conceptual Path

If you want to engage directly with the ideas. What is the Theory of Forms? Why does Plato distrust democracy? What did he believe about the soul?

Explore Philosophy Section →

PATH 3

The Textual Path

If you want to read the dialogues immediately. Start with accessible, shorter works and experience Socratic questioning firsthand.

Recommended starting dialogues:

  • Apology (30 min) — Socrates' defense at trial
  • Crito (20 min) — Should he escape prison?
  • Meno (40 min) — How do we learn?
Read Apology (MIT) →

Or wait for: Talk with Plato (coming soon)

There is no wrong choice

These paths are not mutually exclusive. You can start with history, switch to concepts, then read a dialogue. The site is designed to support whatever approach works best for you.

What matters is that you engage seriously—that you read slowly, think carefully, and allow yourself to be puzzled. Philosophy is not a body of facts to memorize. It is a practice to cultivate.
